Why Solve a ReCaptcha To Connect?

ReCaptcha: Love it or hate it, Google's little gray rectangle is everywhere. But what does that omnipresent checkbox really do? Many believe it stops bots, but it in fact does not. Bot developers can easily connect to a captcha solving service like 2captcha and boom, captchas no longer impede the robotic siege. So what does ReCaptcha do and why does it seem to be everywhere? ReCaptcha does a few things very well:

  • Train Google's AI
    When you click on those street signs and fire hydrants, Google sends that into their data-munching machine, compares it with what other users have said, and decides if you are a bot. If you aren't, they then can use that information (This photo is a Car) to teach their computers how to recognize that thing.

  • Contribute to exploitation
    Not only is using the time and energy of billions of users that don't have a choice in the matter inherently exploitive, the proliferation of captchas has lead to the establishment of services like the aforementioned 2captcha which pay people fractions of a cent for long and unrewarding work.

  • Lock out legitimate users
    ReCaptcha also discriminates against people using the Tor browser, a powerful tool for those who need privacy from anyone from abusers to nation-state hackers. Sometimes this occurs with an false message stating that malware is on the user's network, sometimes the captcha instead goes into the dreaded "Slow Fade" mode, a simulation of which is next to this text. ReCaptcha also locks out people with vision issues. They claim to have an audio captcha, but in practice if you use it you will be banned and given message saying your network is infected.

What can I do about this?

  • Users
    Share this page with the people who run the apps, websites, and services you use

  • Developers
    Switch to another bot stopping system. Develop a threat model, who are you trying to stop? If you want to stop bots that scan the internet for forms to spam, a simple math captcha is likely enough. They are easy to solve, accessible, and will stop casual crawlers from spamming your forms. If you want to prevent targeted attacks from ever touching your site, I'm afraid you are out of luck. The best you can do is slow them down. This is what ReCaptcha does, however there are good alternatives.

    • hCaptcha hCaptcha is an image-recognition captcha system similar to ReCaptcha. hCaptcha is slightly better, with their stated mission including privacy protection, but they don't have a good accessibility system.

    • Friendly Captcha
      Friendly Captcha doesn't rely on image recognition, instead having the user's device do about 20 seconds of math. This occurs in the background while the user is filling the form, so the experience is not interrupted for legitimate users but the time adds up fast for bots. It is accessible to users that use assistive technology like screen readers.

    • Cryptominers
      This kind of challenge will use the user's computer to mine some amount of cryptocurrency before the form can be submitted. This approach is widely blocked by antivirus and adblockers, and wastes significant time and electricity. Services in this category include hashcash.io, Crypto Loot, and the now defunct Coinhive. I don't recommend the use of these.
